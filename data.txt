1. Preventing Choking

To reduce the risk of choking, chew food slowly and thoroughly before swallowing. Avoid talking or laughing while eating. Cut food into small pieces for children, and keep hard candies, nuts, and small objects out of reach of toddlers. When serving high-risk foods such as grapes or hot dogs, slice them into smaller, safer portions. Encourage older adults to take small bites and sip fluids with meals.

2. Treating Minor Cuts and Scrapes

First wash your hands to avoid introducing infection. Clean the wound gently with running water; use mild soap around but not inside the cut. Apply pressure with a clean cloth or bandage if bleeding persists. Once bleeding slows, apply an antiseptic or antibiotic ointment and cover with a sterile dressing. Replace the bandage daily or when it becomes wet or dirty.

3. Managing Burns (Mild/First-Degree)

Immediately cool the burn under cool (not ice-cold) running water for at least 10–15 minutes. Remove any jewelry or tight clothing near the area before swelling begins. After cooling, cover the burn with a sterile, non-fluffy dressing or clean cloth. Avoid applying butter, oils, or toothpaste. Seek medical attention if the burn is larger than three inches, affects the face, hands, feet, or genitals, or if blisters are severe.
